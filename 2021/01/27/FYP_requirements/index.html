Presentation

Presentation script

Hello everyone, my name is yuxuan wu and thank you for the time in listening my presentation. Today my topic is about m6A RNA modification related to virus, and I would utilize weakly supervised learning, specifically multi-instance learning with gated attention mechanism to identify the potential modification site.

Here is the outline of my whole presentation. I would firstly introduce some background knowledge related to RNA modification as well as neural network. Then I would state the main hypothesis of my project. Next, I would analyze the methods as well as the regarding results and discussion. The final part would be the acknowledgement.

Now we move to the first part, the introduction part. I would focus on both m6a modification as well as neural network. M6a is a non-cap reversible marker present on eukaryotic messenger RNA, a typical, common motif would be DRACH motif. Many biological functions, such as translational efficiency, RNA stability and virus infection demonstrated strong correlation with the modification on m6a, which indicates the huge potential in this area. And I would like to mentioned that the virus infection is my focus in this project. However, traditional wet experiments have two major drawbacks, either time consuming or high investment, therefore, the analysis based on neural networks in silico would be an ideal choice to replace the traditional analysis. This part is a general view about the model I used in my project. I utilized weakly supervised learning deep neural network for the identification of m6a virus infection site. And specifically, the combination of multi-instance learning as well as gated attention mechanisms enabled me to extract the intrinsic connection among the nucleotides and impart weighted value to each instances during training process.

2.00

In this slides, I would illustrate the main hypothesis of my project, that is to find out whether the infection of virus would correlate with the RNA medication of m6a site. The following three points motivated me to analyze the m6a modification with virus infection, firstly, there are limited models based on virus genome; secondly, the existing models have poor generalization abilities. Furthermore, the fast identification of virus would be a requirement, especially during the pandemic attack.

3:00

Now we move to the materials and methods part, I would state the process of data preparation and model building. In this scenario, 9 virus were included, including the covid19 virus. The sample transcripts were obtained from exomePeak2 via peak calling, and preprocessed by Senior PHD kunqi. I then used R to ensure the equal peak width in both positive and negative samples at each sequence length because only in this way, we could avoid the bias caused by the inequality of the sequence length. Noted that the positive samples were denoted as the sites where virus infection could have RNA modification and negative samples are the opposite. Moreover, in this project, I took both sequence as well as structure information of the RNA sequence into consideration. The structure information was generated by RNAfold package and I would analyze it in detail later.

4min

In this slides, I introduced the weakly-supervised RNA modification model, also abbreviated as WeakRM model. This model was inspired by PhD student Daiyun. I would separate this model into three sections embedding, encoding and training. The first embedding part was achieved by a sliding window with fixed length to convert the whole sequence into a bag with multiple instances and fed into the second encoding part. In this part one-hot method transformed each nucleotide into a four times 1 vector. The third part would be the combination of gated attention mechanisms as well as the bag level classifier to calculate the probability of each sequence. The reason for applying this weakly-supervised learning model was to extract as much information as possible based on the limited samples.

5 min

Additionally, from literature review, I found that the structure information of the sequence data may also play an active role in determining the modification site. As a result, with the assistance of RNAfold package, six symbols were applied to represent the situation of structure information treated with same one-hot encoding and concatenate the encoding data to the bag level classifier.

Furthermore, as the sudden attack of covid19 pandemic, the fast identification of virus would be a requirement in the future, which means the model should have robust and great generalization ability during prediction. Like this figure, the models could generate a series of probability to indicate the most similar species among the existing virus and develop the regarding treatment based on the model prediction. Only in this way, we could reduce the spread of the pandemic and flatten the curve

Now, we move to the results and discussion section. This slide was the performance metrics of nine virus. I would consider AUROC as our focus. We could see that except for HIV-1 and HCV virus, all the other viruses could achieve the auROC higher than 0.80. The most accurate and well-performed scenario is in the prediction of KSHV virus infection, while the detection on HIV-1 virus may not be as superior as the others.

This slide is a little summary of the previous table, the average accuracy of nine virus would be 0.75 and KSHV achieve highest performance and HIV was the lowest.

This slide is the comparison with structure information included. I made a bar chart to illustrate the comparison of one sample. The red type is the sequence and structure based performance while blue type is sequence information only. We could see from the AUROC of each virus, no significant difference observed with the inclusion of structure information. Thus, I could conclude that the structure information played a little role in the RNA modification in this case.

This slide is the measurement of generalization ability in model. I utilized heat map of the auROC value under different virus training model. The darker the value, the higher the correlation. The model demonstrates overall robust performance in the identification, but HIV-1 and Covid19 are two exceptions. I therefore hypothesized that these two virus share limited similarities with existing virus, which therefore to some extent explains the lack of efficient treatment towards these two diseases.

The final part would be the acknowledgement part. I would like to show great gratitude towards my superior Dr Jia Meng. Without his support and guidance, I could not finish the project successfully. I also would like to say thank you to my senior PhD student, daiyun and kunqi. They offered me great help in data preparation and model building. That’s all for my presentation, thank you for listening.

  • Organization
  • Content quality of visual aids

Structure of the FYP talk

  • Introduction
  • Hypothesis (Motivation / Biological questions)
    • Objectives and project outlines
  • Results (including methods and interpretation)
  • Conclusion and summary (including a “big picture” of the research field)
  • Acknowledgement

Introduction

  • Greeting audience
  • Introduce the topic
  • Provide relevant background information (add the citation)

Hypothesis (Biological questions)

  • Logical and reasonable flow from your introduction
  • Clearly state the hypothesis you will talking about

Objectives and project outline

  • Outline the project and the methods involved

Results (including methods and interpretation)

  • Explain purpose and methodology used
  • Make an appropriate conclusion
  • Logical connection to the next result slide
  • Be concise

Conclusion and summary

  • Summarize your findings
  • Make appropriate concluding remarks
  • Refer to the significance in the context of the research field
  • Provide a working model based on your results

Acknowledgement

  • Acknowledge peoples involved in the project
  • Acknowledge funding bodies

Q & A

Always repeat the question

  1. For you to make sure you understood it
  2. For audience to make sure they heard it

Don’t be afraid of saying “I don’t know”

Report

General requirements:

Maximum word length (excluding abstract, figure legends, reference list and appendices) 5000 words.

Double spaced

Font 12 point, black

Binding/e-versions (must be as described below)

Scientific English in appropriate tense

Free of grammar, punctuation and spelling errors

Contain the following, in this order;

  1. Completed cover template

  2. Abstract in Chinese and in English

  3. Introduction

  4. Methods

  5. Results and Discussion or Results/Discussion as a single section

  6. Acknowledgements (if appropriate)

  7. Reference list

  8. Appendices (only if appropriate) see below

Report must conform at least to the SBS style guides with respect to the following:

Article citations within the text

Reference list

Symbols, units, abbreviations

Accurate syntax for organism names

Organism specific rules for genetic loci

International rules for molecular nomenclature

Tables

Figures

Figure legends

Details

  1. Maximum 5000 words (total word length should be included on the title page) for the main text i.e. introduction; methods; results; discussion and any text in footnotes. Note that this is a maximum, not an expectation with a margin of variation; concise reports shorter than this may be entirely appropriate.
  2. Word processed; 12 point Times Roman (black); double spaced. Colour in figures is acceptable, but consider whether colour is really needed to illustrate what you require. Only use colour where it will increase legibility.
  3. The hard copy should be on A4 paper with a cover sheet consisting of a completed project cover template and should be produced as booklets, preferably with a plastic cover and submitted in person to the department secretary by 11 am on Monday 4 of May (Week 12), with a copy of your Ethical Approval, if appropriate. You will be required to sign a sheet to confirm that your report contains no instances of plagiarism, collusion or fabrication
  4. The electronic version (including any appendix) should be submitted on the module’s ICE page before the 11 am on Monday 4 May (Week 12), in the form of a Microsoft Word file (.doc or *.docx) using the following file format *SURNAME_FIRST NAME_ID_NUMBER_FR**
  5. t is usual to write about the work performed in the past tense and results are usually presented in the passive form
  6. sub-headings may help readability.
  7. Define abbreviations at the point in the text where they are first used

Components

Title:

  • Choose a title that is concise but informative
  • Ideally it should describe the work performed and findings but not be dramatic

Abstracts:

  • The abstract should be provided in Chinese and in English
  • Maximum words 250
  • Should not include references
  • Should condense the aims, observations, findings and main conclusions

Introduction:

  • Provide background (e.g. literature, preliminary data) to the field of study
  • State the purpose of the work e.g., the scientific hypothesis
  • Provide the rationale for the approach taken

Materials and Methods:

  • Should NOT be a protocol book.
  • Should be sufficiently detailed for the work to be repeatable.
  • Should be written in a format appropriate for publication in a journal relevant to the type of work performed.
  • Should refer back to previous Key Skills work.

Results

  • Present analyses, not raw data, including appropriate and fully explained statistical analyses.
  • Each result should be accompanied by text explaining the rationale for the experiment/observation; a succinct description of the method used to obtain the data; the methods used to analyse/interpret the data and sufficient introductory comments and discussion to link the results into a coherent narrative.
  • Sub-headings may be appropriate.

Tables must:

  • Be numbered consecutively according to their appearance in the text (Table 1 etc.).
  • Have short informative titles and legends placed above the Table.
  • All tables must be referred to in the text.
  • Explanatory material may be included as footnotes immediately below the table e.g. definitions of all acronyms and abbreviations.

Figures must:

  • Be numbered consecutively according to their appearance in the text (Figure 1 etc.).
  • Have an accompanying title and legend below the Figure.
  • Have a uniform lettering style.
  • Consider putting extensive sequences and alignments or other data in an appendix

Figure legends must:

  • Be intelligible to the reader without reference to the text.

  • Define abbreviations or symbols used in the Figure.

Discussion should:

  • Critically review the findings.
  • Indicate whether the findings support the hypothesis.
  • Provide some speculative debate.
  • Discuss possible future experiments.

Acknowledgments:

  • Should be at the end of the text.

  • Should acknowledge contributions from others e.g. other lab/School members that have aided the work in any way.

Reference:

Harvard

Symbols, units, abbreviations:

  • Nonstandard abbreviations must be defined at first occurrence

  • Species and generic names must be italicized scientific binomens but generic names that are also common names should not be italicized when used alone e.g. Drosophila melanogaster and Drosophila [Drosophila melanogaster is incorrect]. D. melanogaster is acceptable for subsequent uses, provided no ambiguity would result.

  • Genetic loci must be italicized and must follow the established rules of genetic nomenclature for the organism in question

  • The International Union of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology rules of nomenclature must be followed for amino acids, peptides, proteins, enzymes, nucleic acids, polynucleotides, carbohydrates, and lipids; these can currently be found at http://www.chem.qmul.ac.uk/iubmb/nomenclature/
