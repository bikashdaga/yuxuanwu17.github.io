m6A-Atlas ^1

N6-methyladenosine (m6A):

non-cap reversible marker present on eukaryotic messenger RNAs (mRNAs) and long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs)

Roles & functions

  • virus infection (escaping from recognition by the RNA sensor)
  • stress
  • heat shock
  • DNA damage
  • regulates molecular functions such as RNA–protein interaction
  • RNA stability
  • translation efficiency

collection of virus m6a sites:

m6a sites located on viral transcripts were detected by exomePeak2

How to quntify the m6a methylation levels:

fold enrichment of reads in the IP samples compared with the input control samples within the 200 bp window of a given m6A site. (related to peak-calling process that usually seeks genomic regions with fold enrichment of >1 at a pre-defined siginificance level)

Potential involvement of individual m6A sites in pathogenesis

Inspired by m6AVar **, the potential involvement of individual m6A sites in pathogenesis was inferred from disease associated genetic mutations that directly destroy the m6A forming motif **DRACH.(=elimination of an m6A site)

Results:

m6A sites on viral transcripts from 10 virus species during 8 infection stages, including, hMPV, KSHV, HCV, RSV, HIV-1, DENV,WNV, ZIKV,HCMVand YFV. Analysis revealed a total of 5388 genetic mutations, linked
m6A to pathogenesis, which can destroy the forming motif m6A DRACH of these reliable sites, potentially indirectly m6A linking function to 134 different types of disease

GraphProt

RBPs

The gold standard for identifying RBP targets are experimental cross-linking immunoprecipitation-high-throughput sequencing (CLIP-seq) protocols

Limitations of the current methods

  • the data may contain many false positives due to inherent noise
  • a large number of binding sites remain unidentified (a high false negative rate)
    • CLIP-seq is sensitive to expression levels and is both time and tissue dependent
    • limited mappability
    • mapping difficulties at splice sites lead to further false negatives, even on highly expressed mRNAs
    • The resulting data requires non-trivial peak detection to control for false positives. Peak detection leads to high-fidelity binding sites; however, it again increases the number of false negatives.

Multi-instance learning

大道至简—-多示例学习与注意力机制的巧妙结合（Attention-based deep multiple instance learning）中文笔记

机器学习：多示例学习MIL

Definition：

  1. Multi-instance learning (MIL) is a variation of supervised learning where a single class label is assigned to a bag of instance.
  2. MIL deal with a bag of instances for which a single class label is assigned
  3. A DNA sequence can be considered as positive bag if there is at least one instance containing TFBS; a negative bag: no any instance containing the TFBS

Goals

  • Learn a model that predicts a bag level
  • Discover key instances (instance that trigger the bag label)

This paper

(i) a transformation of instances to a low dimensional embedding
(ii) a permutation-invariant (symmetric) aggregation function

(iii) a final transformation to the bag probability

Theroem 1: weights together with the embeddings are parts of the function

In details:

  1. A transformation of instances using the function f
  2. A combination of transformed instances using a symmetric function for pooling (MIL pooling)
  3. A transformation of combined instances transformed by f using a function g
  4. The expressive of the score function relies on the choice of classes of functions for f and g, is the final probability

Two approaches in MIL

  1. Instance-level approach:

    • f is an instance level classier that returns the instance of each instance
    • Individual scores are aggregated by pooling to obtain the final probability
    • Function g is a identity function (no influence)

  2. Embedding-level approach:(preferable for its superior performance)

    • Function f maps instances to a low-dimensional embedding
    • MIL pooling obtains a bag representation that is independent of the number of instances in the bag
    • The bag representation is further processed by a bag-level classifier to provide the final probability

  3. reasons for method 2 preferred:

    1. Unkown instances, may be insufficiently trained

    2. Determines a joint representation of a bag – not introduce additional bias to the bag-level classifier

==Noted that:== Modified embedding approach could find the key instance, which is a feature owned by instance-level approach

MIL pooling layer

  • Aggregate either instance probability distribution vectors or instance features vectors into a bag feature vector (bridges MI data with conventional neural network)
  • Pooling was used to ensure the permutation-invariant
  • Differentiable

MIL Pooling method selection

  1. Max
  2. Mean
  3. Noisy-and

Pooling disadvantage:

  1. Pre-defined and not trainable
  2. Not flexible and applicable in previously mentioned methods (instance-based or embedding-based)
  3. Not interpretable

Attention mechanism

  • A weighted average of instance (low-dimensional embeddings), where weights are determined by a neural network
  • Weights must sum to 1 to ensure the permutation invariance (exclude the influence of the bag size)

Difference with traditional attention mechanisms:

  • Traditional: instances are sequentially dependent
  • Here: all instances are independent

Gated attention mechanisms

  • Tanh()’s nonlinearity is not sufficient to learn complex relations. Range from [-1,1], which could limit the final expression of learned relations among instance
  • Add a new sigmoid function with element-wise multiplication
  • The gating mechanisms introduce a learnable non-linearity that potentially removes the troublesome linearity in tanh()

Let’s compare with the softmax function, we could find that ak could simply be the multiplication of tanh()*sigma()

Advantages

  1. Flexibility:
    1. the pooling mechanisms allow to assign different weights to instances within a bag and hence the final representation of the bag could be highly informative for the bag-level classifier, could find the key instance.
    2. Application of the attention-based MIL pooling with the transformation of function f and g makes the whole model differentiable
  2. interpretability:
    1. Y (bag level = 1), key instance should be 1
    2. It bridges the instance-leave approach and the embedding-level approach.

Difference between other methods

  • we propose to replace widely used permutation-invariant operators such as the maximum operator max and the mean operator mean by a trainable weighted average where weights are given by a two-layered neural network.
  • Two-layered neural networks corresponds to the attention mechanisms
  • Attention weights could help to find the key instances

[^4]: permutation invariant:

In this context this refers to the fact that the model does not assume any spatial relationships between the features. E.g. for multilayer perceptron, you can permute the pixels and the performance would be the same. This is not the case for convolutional networks, which assume neighborhood relations. WSCNN

This paper propose a weakly-supervised convolutional neural network architecture (WSCNN), combining multiple-instance learning (MIL) with CNN, to further boost the performance of predicting protein-DNA binding. WSCNN first divides each DNA sequence into multiple overlapping subsequences (instances) with a sliding window, and then separately models each instance using CNN, and finally fuses the predicted scores of all instances in the same bag using four fusion methods, including Max, Average, Linear Regression, and Top-Bottom Instances.

Supervised learning model limitation

  1. Poor ability to handle the large scale of sequence data
  2. Poor generalization ability
  3. Time-consuming
  4. …

Image classification field

  • Each image may contain multiple objects of the interest
  • MIL-based model first extract candidate regions (instances) with existing segmentation mechanisms
  • Separately model each instance with the existing deep CNN
  • Fuse the scores of all instances in the same bag using the frequent-used MAX method

MIL (sequence field)

TF-bind sequence may contain multiple TFBSs.

  • Divide DNA sequences (with length l) into multiple overlapping subsequences (instances, with starting point shift s) with a sliding window (with length c).
  • All possible instances in the whole sequence are considered as a bag labeled positive or negative according to the binding signal value
  • The number of instance per bag is [(l-c)/s]+1, where c and s are two hyper-parameters
  • Each instance is mapped to a feature vector representing k-mer approaches
  • Model all possible instances with the model
  • Fuse the predicted scores of all instance in the same bag using the previous stated methods

Why not directly use advanced pooling layers to combine MIL with CNN?

  • A regular CNN would perform some kind of MIL
  • DNA sequence with limited length could not be guaranteed to contain enough TFBS, which may lead to little effect of using advanced pooling functions

Benefits using sliding window to divide the sequence into multiple instances:

  1. The weakly-supervised information are maintained
  2. Enough instance containing TFBSs are generated
  3. Implicitly taking into binding site location consideration

Future improvements

  1. One-hot encoding assumes all nucleotides in the binding sites are statistically independent
  2. RNN could capture long-term dependency
  3. Noisy-and pooling function achieved higher performance, other application specific pooling method could come out to conduct the further analysis

WSCNNLSTM

  • Add a LSTM network
  • Add a K-mer encoding method
  • Use noisy and to fuse the score

Convolutional neural network (CNN) is a special version of article neural network which adopts a weight-sharing strategy to capture local patterns in data such as DNA sequences.

Recurrent neural network (RNN) is another variant of ANN where connections between neurons form a directed graph. Unlike CNN, RNN can use its internal state (memory) to exhibit dynamic temporal or spatial behavior. In the designed model, the convolution layer is used to capture motif features, while the recurrent layer is used to capture long-term dependencies between the motif features.

Improvements

  1. WSCNN mainly employed CNN to learn motif features from DNA sequences, and did not take into consideration the long-term dependencies between motif features. In the weakly supervised framework, therefore we add a bi-directional recurrent layer after the convolutional layer to capture the forward and backward long-term dependencies between motif features.
  2. WSCNN attempted to use four fusion methods to fuse the predicted values of all instances in a bag, and then selected the best one of them as the final prediction. However, it is inconvenient for user to decide which one is better, so they have to try the four fusion methods one by one. Therefore we offer a better and more robust fusion method Noisy-and to replace them.

K-mer encoding:

One-hot encoding ignores the high-order dependencies among nucleotides

2-mer encoding: take into consideration the dependencies between two adjacent nucleotides

MIL again:

MIL is commonly based on an assumption that a bag is labeled as positive if there is at least one instance that contains TFBS, and is labeled as negative if there are no any instances that contain TFBS

Max only focuses on the most informative instance and overlooks other instances that may contain useful information

Average and Linear Regression take advantage of all information, inevitably containing useless information, and Top-Bottom Instances needs to manually determine the number of the highest and lowest scoring instances.Moreover, how to effectively take advantage of abundant positive instances is also a key point. Thus we adopt a better and more elegant fusion method, named Noisy-and, in this paper.

Noisy-and, which is based on a different assumption that a bag is labeled as positive if the number of positive instances in the bag exceeds a threshold.

Choice between sigmoid and softmax function:

difference between sigmoid or softmax

Binary: almost no difference but preferred sigmoid

Multi-label: softmax, since it ensure all the possibilities sum to 1

Feature importance scores：

The quantification values of the contributions of features to a current model prediction. The simplest way to obtain this score is to perturb the feature value and measure the change in the model prediction: the larger the change found, the more important the feature is.

Two methods for explanation

Perturbation-based approaches systematically perturb the input features and observe the change in the output

  • high computational cost

Backpropagationq-based approach

The simplest backpropagation-based importance scores are saliency maps (gradient absolute values) [^2]
and input-masked gradients (input*gradient) [^3]

[^2]: Saliency maps

Feature importance scores defined as the gradient absolute values of the mode output with respect to the model input.

[^3]: Input-masked gradients：

Feature importance scores defined as the gradient of the model output with respect to the model input multiplied by the input values.

Tensorflow tutorial in interpretability (integrated_gradients)

left: Your model’s gradients for pixel x are positive between 0.0 and 0.8 but go to 0.0 between 0.8 and 1.0. Pixel x clearly has a significant impact on pushing your model toward 80% predicted probability on the true class. Does it make sense that pixel x‘s importance is small or discontinuous?

right: The intuition behind IG is to accumulate pixel x‘s local gradients and attribute its importance as a score for how much it adds or subtracts to your model’s overall output class probability. You can break down and compute IG in 3 parts:

  1. interpolate small steps along a straight line in the feature space between 0 (a baseline or starting point) and 1 (input pixel’s value)
  2. compute gradients at each step between your model’s predictions with respect to each step
  3. approximate the integral between your baseline and input by accumulating (cumulative average) these local gradients

Deeplift

Description: Deeplift compares the activation of each neuron to its ‘reference activation’ and assigns contribution scores according to the difference

The reference is the activation that the neuron has when the network is provided a ‘reference input’ (the reference input is determined according to what is appropriate for the task at hand, domain knowledge)

Deeplift considers the deviation from a neuron’s reference activity, making it capable of handling RNN memory units gated by activations that have vanishing gradients (eg:sigmoid, tanh)

==w.r.t. (with respect to)==

Previous works

  1. Perturbation based approach
    • Saturation problems
    • Computational inefficiency
  2. Backpropagation-based approach
    • When back propagating importance using gradients, the gradient coming into a ReLU during the backward pass is zero would out if the input to the ReLU during the forward pass is negative (statement)
    • Guided Backpropagation:
      • Equivalent to computing gradients, with the caveat that any gradients that become negative during the backward pass are discarded at ReLus
      • Due to the zero-ing out of negative gradients, both guided back propagation and deconvolutional networks can fail to highlight inputs that contribute negatively to the output
    • Still could not solve the saturation problem
  3. Layer wise relevance propagation and gradientinput
    1. LRP: absent modifications to deal with numerical stability
    2. The LRP runs for ReLU networks were equivalent within a scaling factor to an element wise product between the saliency maps and the input
    3. Could not run on a FPU
    4. Unable to solve the saturation problem and thresholding artifacts
  4. Integrated gradients
    • In stead of computing the gradients at only the current value of the input, one can integrate the gradients as the inputs are scaled up from some starting value to their current value
    • Address the saturation and thresholding problems; but numerically obtaining high quality integrals adds computational overhead

Why unique:

  1. Frame the question of importance in terms of difference from a reference state, where the reference is chosen according to the problem at hand
    • In contrast to most gradient based methods, using a difference-from-reference allows Deeplift to propagate an importance signal even in situations where the gradient is zero and avoids artifacts caused by discontinuities in the gradient
    • By optionally giving separate consideration to the effects of positive and negative contributions at nonlinearities, DeepLift can reveal dependencies missed by other approaches
      • Deeplift scores are computed using a backpropagation-like algorithms, which could be obtained efficiently in a backward pass after a prediction has made
  • What should I measure differences against?
  • Can study results with multiple different references
  • MNIST: all zeros
  • Genomics
    • Frequencies of ACGT
    • Shuffled sequences: for each sequence, generate reference by shuffling, averaging the results over all the generated references
    • Deepbind (dishuffle) [^5]

Deeplift address the limitation of gradient-based approaches because the difference from the reference may be non-zero even when the gradient is zero.

Current limitation of gradient-based approaches:

  • Activation function such as Relu have a gradient of zero when they are not firing, but a Relu does not fire still contains the information
  • Similarly, the popular activation function of sigmoid or tanh activation are widely utilized as gates in memory units of recurrent neural networks such as GRUs and LSTMs, but these activation have a near-zero gradient at high or low inputs even though such inputs can be very significant

Chain rules for multipliers:

Given the multipliers for each neuron to its immediate successors, we can compute the multipliers for any neuron to a given target neuron efficiently via backpropagation

Define the reference:

For the binary classification tasks on DNA sequence inputs (strings over the alphabet A,C,G,T)

  1. we obtained sensible results using either a reference input containing the expected frequencies of ACGT in the background

  2. by averaging the results over multiple reference inputs for each sequence that are generated by shuffling each original sequence (dinucleotide-shuffling are mostly preferred)

[^5]: why we used dinucleotide shuffling

Why dinucleotide shuffling?

One undesirable aspect of using the frequencies of ACGT as the reference is that such a representation is not a one-hot encoding and thus never occurs in the training data.

Thus, the network may behave unexpectedly on the reference sequence. As an alternative, we can average results over several one-hot encoded sequences generated by shuffling the original sequence. The results are shown below. On real genomic data, a dinucleotide-preserving shuffle would be more appropriate than a random shuffle as CG dinucleotides are underrepresented in genomic sequence due to spontaneous deamination from C to T

CG suppression is a term for the phenomenon that CG dinucleotides are very uncommon in most portions of vertebrate genomes.
